# *Counting Co-inhabitants*

You may recall that, during the process of the second project, I had taken a moment or two to prioritize other work; among that other work was the planning and undertaking of the third field excursion. This field excursion was, of course, intended to gather information with which I might explore the various other inhabitants of the seven places of interest. I began with four overarching concepts to tailor my prompts around: current co-inhabitants (observed during the recollection process), past co-inhabitants (recalled to have  been present in times past), concepts and ideas that were present in each space, and absent or unnoticed co-inhabitants that I might intuit to be present.

As I wrapped my head around such concepts, I had also pondered the incorporation of multiple senses. I intended to explore sounds as possible co-inhabitants in early imaginations of this project, yet might further senses find a place in the recollection process, or in the project more broadly? Ultimately, I decided to limit myself to sights and sounds (some “touch”-related co-inhabitants, such as a soft breeze, could also be represented via sound). Touch, smell, and taste were ruled out. Touch and taste were largely unfeasible, for I could not–and would not–go around attempting to use touch and taste to observe co-inhabitants. This may perhaps be a failure of imagination, but I was unable to determine suitable (and caring) methods that could pinpoint and identify co-inhabitants through such senses. Touch and taste would take on an inappropriately invasive nature in the context of this work. Smell, while far more conducive to caring (and non-invasive) observation, lacks precision (at least without particular training) and would have been just a bit more uncertain than I was comfortable with.

While the unique prompts for this particular recollection process were under consideration, I had once again borrowed the general prompts from earlier iterations of the recollection repository. The contexts for these prompts were updated to match the particular nuances of this third project. I had also resolved to set aside a second possible day for the recollection process, although my field notes do indicate a preference for such an extension to prove unnecessary.

I then turned to the development of the unique prompts for this excursion’s recollection entries. The first prompt requested the observation of presences which were presently present; while presences were first operationalized as concepts, ideas, beings, structures, and sounds, they were later simplified to beings, concepts, and sounds. I had pondered the definition of a “presence” (in the context of this recollection process) and found myself reducing scope in the interest of manageability. My first instinct had been to focus on things that might change and evolve: things that were thus dynamic. As it so happens, a more-than-human approach to space renders all things in that space to be dynamic. This, in turn, rendered such a scope to be meaningless. A line cried out, demanding to be drawn, and I adopted a limited approach to dynamic presences, settling for things that were relatively unfixed. This, for example, ruled out physical infrastructure, yet allowed for changes to that infrastructure to be marked (largely through sound, such as the disarming thud of doors slamming shut). Hence the focus on beings, concepts, and sounds as particular presences to which the excursion would be oriented. I had also raised the possibility of marking my emotional responses to presences, as an avenue for exploring my sense of place through relations to co-inhabitants; this consideration was ultimately cast aside, although my description of some sounds might perhaps hint at such associations.

I had also wished to introduce an element of time and change in the recollection of co-inhabitants, and this was captured through a prompt for past presences. These would be presences that were not observed during the excursion, yet recalled from prior experiences. If the present presences prompt carries with it the issue of observation bias, this second prompt introduces recollection bias and the unreliability of memory. An important theme arose here, variations of which would permeate the entire process of this project: is exhaustive recollection possible? This would later prove to be a consideration that firmly places this project in the realm of subjective place, rather than “objective” space. And, while such concerns are, ultimately, unavoidable, I did construct a prompt for absent or unnoticed presences. This prompt would create space for the emergence of absences that result from–yet are still mediated by–my own situated perspective. There is, of course, a limited extent to which I may recognize absences that I have yet to (knowingly) observe. Yet this prompt would force me to grapple with that which I have yet to notice or may have overlooked. While imperfect, such grappling was understood as an act of care directed toward each space.

A few miscellaneous considerations that emerged during the development of these three primary prompts: overlap between past presences and absences could be possible, and thus I emphasized that absences would be operationalized as *presences that have never been observed by my person*; as with the other recollections, I had determined that the adjustment of recollection entries following the completion of the field excursion would be acceptable; observing “concepts” as presences would be difficult, and may require further research; similarly, the validity of concepts that did emerge “naturally” could be assessed at a later time; I may have to determine the limits of what is or is not present in a space depending on the boundaries I associate with the place of interest; a ponderance of the suitable level of quantification was undergone, leading me to conclude that, while precise numbers would almost certainly be inaccurate (particularly so when attempting to recall past presences, and even when only noting presences which I actively observe), some indicators of numerical patterns could enrich the forthcoming project output.

I had tested the recollection repository–confirming that it was indeed ready for the field excursion–and began to ponder possible components of this third project output. To what extent would I “classify” presences? Is categorization appropriate for emergent and care-oriented exploration? Might I interrogate biases (of myself and others) through the adoption of particular categories? In a similar vein, I had raised the abstract notion of playfully representing my own biases through the use of recollected information. Exclusion–which would be inevitable in this project–could become generative if carefully addressed, or even critiqued.

With a completed (and tested) recollection repository in place, I was ready to embark upon the third and final field excursion. Intriguingly, I found myself loath to return to one of the seven places; while I did not skip it entirely, I decided to walk through, observe my surroundings, and then settle down in the next place to create the corresponding recollection entry. Underlying this decision was an (admittedly dangerous) assumption that this space would be quite similar to the next, with differences having been adequately explored in prior projects; I had also realized that this place carried far less meaning to me than I had imagined when this place emerged during the first field excursion. As always, a familiar, yet now heightened, uncertainty lingered: will I observe and recall everything that I should?

Fortunately, the excursion itself went rather smoothly. I had found that this process was rather tiring, and I noticed that my focus began to slip after the first entry. Yet rushed I was not; I even took care to spend more time than I had needed to observe my surroundings. No emotions were recollected/observed, but some concepts did emerge. I questioned whether the prompt for absences was particularly valuable during the excursion process, eventually acknowledging that facing the question of absences while present in the space was itself an important act–even if not all of the noted absences felt particularly insightful or perspective-altering. Sounds abound among the recollected presences, although it became quite clear to me that sound could not be reproduced with much accuracy. I made a copy of the recollection entries, and kept space open for any excursion-related ideas and reflections that may have arisen at a later point.

Following the excursion and the all-important post-excursion reflection, my attention returned to the second project. Once the second project draft had been completed, and my start-of-academic-year preparations were wrapped, the third project took flight once more. My return to this project began with an exploration of possible project ideas and designs. Charts were front of mind here as I pondered what the vague and imprecise quantitative data I had collected might lend itself too. A sunburst chart struck me as a flexible representation of information across various degrees of quantification. Previous familiarity with such a representational technique was certainly at play, too, and I surmised that I could create a chart of each place of interest. Yet I was uncertain as to how I might resist hierarchical ordering in my use of the humble sunburst chart. Categorization, after all, would run counter to an underlying desire to honor a nonanthropocentric approach to space.

My attention then turned to the imagination of a possible map of co-inhabitants. Might I use pins or icons to place co-inhabitants on a map? But how would I actually place them at representative points? Pins and coordinates would have been far too rigid to adequately honor the dynamic agency of many co-inhabitants; how else might I represent things in space? The choice of basemap style led to a similar conundrum: basemaps, created for human use, are overwhelmingly anthropocentric. A further complication: how might I represent sound, via text and on a map? How does one place sound at a coordinate point?

With these unresolved challenges in mind, I returned to the recollected information, looking to evaluate what I had access to and thus might represent. I listed patterns in the recollected presences, including perceived absences, visual and auditory presences (both observed and remembered), various “types” of presences (although I was hesitant to rely too heavily on categories), and, rather playfully, indications of my own presence. I resolved to include myself among the presences as I put together this project. Rather than forcing possible additions to the recollected information, I had decided to leave the recollected information as-is unless further recollections were to emerge naturally. While exploring the recollected information and identifying patterns held within was indeed a helpful exercise, certainty continued to elude me.

Truly, this was not a time for certainty, for ideas of a remarkably abstract nature had begun to emerge. Might I use the underlying motivations of this research to aid in the structure of this project? In this way, the project structure itself would reflect the themes of this work. How might I represent care, and being in kinship with others, through design choices? Might I “break” conventional GIS design language, in the name of care? I pondered the possibility of a noncartographic GIS that emphasized relationality, realizing that, while I may not know what such a GIS would look like, I could adopt a project design that features deeply interconnected–and thus at least somewhat relational–components…

Fortunately, knowing that I intended to use both charts and some sort of map component in my exploration of co-inhabitants and place had led me to identify a dashboard as a suitable project structure (and one with the possibility to emphasize relationality through interconnected components). Places were imagined to be explored via a context panel when selected, and would each be associated with a unique sunburst chart. Presences within each place might then be represented via thematic pins on a map panel, each presence featuring a pop-up with additional information. Each place could feature its own subset of data; while these subsets were, at first, imagined to be organized within a single dataset, I had eventually found it more convenient for each place to be associated with a distinct dataset. While many of these dashboard features would eventually be reimagined, this relatively clear picture represented the first tangible vision for this project.

I was not quite satisfied with this initial inkling, for a few reasons. Underlying themes, around which I had considered designing the project, were suspiciously absent. To further represent nonanthropocentric and relational thinking, might I use cartographic principles to situate spaces, but noncartographic techniques to explore them? This led me to situate each place on a cartographic map via a broad polygon, rationalizing that doing so would represent the ambiguities of place and resist anthropocentric boundaries. I also determined that I would not “pin” anything at particular locations, for co-inhabitants are dynamic agents free to move within and beyond a given space. Noncartographic exploration, then, could occur in a context panel, placed side-by-side with the map panel. Could I also emphasize relationality through a carefully balanced dashboard design and layout? This consideration had motivated me to carefully plan the relative positioning of dashboard elements and their interactions with one another.

While pondering how to situate the places on the map, I had realized that I ought to situate myself on it, too. I had, after all, been situated in a specific location during each recollection; this certainly influenced the outcomes of my recollections. Further, by situating myself on the map, I could draw attention to the situated nature of what may seem like an objective “counting” exercise. Opting to use a pin to clearly position myself within each space, I decided to place a self-icon at the coordinates corresponding to the positions at which I had conducted the recollections. This self-icon would appear only when the corresponding place’s polygon was selected by the user, in an effort to minimize the underlying anthropocentricism of such self-inclusion.

On a related note, I had begun to question the role of “place”, as a concept, in this project. Might “place” center my perspectives, needs, and desires over those of the space? In other words, I realized that there may be tensions between the exploration of place and nonanthropocentric values. Beyond–but also related to–this tension, I was also concerned that this particular GIS project might have been too far removed from the exploration of place. While I felt reasonably confident in an assertion that place is indeed implicit in an exploration of my relations to co-inhabitants, an anxiety regarding the role of place in this project would persist.

Yet, despite these anxieties, I felt that I had a strong enough vision to begin the practical development of the project. To quickly develop the necessary code for this project, I carefully crafted an AI prompt. The finalized prompt, submitted to Microsoft Copilot, was as follows:

> Hello! I’m working on a web GIS project, and I’d like some help putting the structure together. I’m looking to create a dashboard organized into three panels. This will be done using the Leaflet library. The first panel would feature the map, the second panel would feature information that corresponds to the items that are selected on the map panel, and the third panel would offer some background information for the dashboard. The first panel would be on the right half of the web page, the second panel would be on the left side, and the third panel would be placed below the both of them, along the bottom of the page. This GIS dashboard will incorporate a GeoJSON dataset to represent locations and their properties. These properties will include a “location” property, which will store a string, a “context” property, which will also store a string, and a “positionality” property, which will store coordinate points. The map panel will visualize the locations in the dataset as polygons. When the polygon is hovered over by the user, the polygon will be highlighted and the “context” property will appear as a pop-up. When the polygon is clicked on by the user, the polygon will also be highlighted, and a custom icon will appear at the “positionality” property’s coordinates. When a polygon is clicked on by the user, the context panel will check the “location” property of the selected polygon, and will present predetermined contextual information depending on the value of this “location” property. The value of this “location” property will also determine which locally-stored csv file will be referenced when constructing the second key piece of this context panel: A plotly sunburst chart, developed with javascript. For our purposes, I will create this sunburst chart at a later time, but I would appreciate it if a code structure that sets up a variable to store a corresponding csv file depending on the polygon that was selected was still added to our web page. The third panel will simply include background information, and will use placeholder values for now. And while this is just a first draft of this web GIS dashboard, it would be important to include a land acknowledgement. I can and will refine the land acknowledgement later; I would just like you to add one for now! I appreciate the help, and let me know if I need to offer further clarification. Thanks!

The initial version of this prompt had requested the use of the MapLibre GL JavaScript library, resulting in output code that was simply woeful. Adjusting to the Leaflet library instead resulted in substantially more refined code and design, although a few manual changes to the output code were necessary. A few things to note about this prompt: in order to preserve my intended vision for the project, and to define for myself the finalized vision of what I wished to create, I had determined quite a few design and implementation details prior to submitting the prompt (although, interestingly, the colors and fonts to be used by the dashboard were left ambiguous); a land acknowledgement was included from the very beginning of the dashboard practical development–yet I would argue that using AI to draft a land acknowledgement almost entirely misses the point of including one, even if the intention was to write a project-specific one later (although I did hope to at least raise the importance of a land acknowledgement to this particular chatbot); finally, my vision for the datasets are made clear here as well. This intended use of multiple datasets, including seven CSVs used to store information about each place that will be used in the diagram/context panel, and the single GeoJSON that would be used to represent the polygons for each place (and my positionality within those places!), was intended to allow for greater flexibility when crafting and applying these datasets.

With the dashboard structure now in place, my first move was to begin the implementation of the self-icon feature, followed by some light critique. Regarding the self-icon, I had ensured that the feet of my self-icon would correspond to the precise coordinates of where I had recollected. As for critique: in my field notes, I had remarked that this current dashboard style felt *too* rigid, *too* refined. Did the dashboard code emerge in this style because I had specified to Copilot that I intended for the project to be a dashboard, a structure that is generally constructed upon positivist foundations? While I had noted the necessity of reworking the dashboard style to something softer and less refined, I never followed through. Fortunately, the color scheme that I later adopted for the sunburst diagram and map layer added some additional color and gentle liveliness to the dashboard.

As the dashboard structure became real, and possibilities quickly dwindled, I once again returned to the theme of relationality. Elements of a dashboard that interact with one another may indeed be a step in a relational direction, but might these elements blend together further? Collapse hierarchies further? Might all elements of the dashboard be on equal footing? Similarly, I found myself uncomfortable with conventional approaches to GIS in this project–yet this discomfort seemed to have more to do with the map components than the dashboard itself. I wondered if I was relying too much on digital technologies; if more open-ended possibilities might emerge were I to put pen-to-paper or try out other means of map construction. The ponderings did not end there, for I realized that I had yet to grapple with temporality in this project design.

Putting such considerations aside, I began to construct a GeoJSON file that would store information about the seven places. To do so, I created a text document in which I could plan and organize potential data features, and, eventually, use such features to construct a GeoJSON file via geojson.io. Through geojson.io, I had defined the associated polygons for each place. For those polygons, I had opted to use circles; circles were chosen as polished and ambiguous representations of space. As for the data features, “location” stored the place-name, “positionality” stored the coordinates at which I made the corresponding recollection entry, and “context”, while first envisioned to offer a comprehensive list of all recollected/observed co-inhabitants, stored a far more manageable sentence that concisely described a primary relation to the corresponding place.

I then turned to the development of the sunburst charts, starting with the development of a CSV file for each of the seven places. I had settled on time and absence as the themes through which I would organize the charts, resulting in three categories: present presences, past presences, and absent or unnoticed presences. One might recall that these three categories seem to map directly onto the recollection prompts, and, while it was a bit of a roundabout process to get to this point, one would indeed be correct. Time allowed for various things to be placed together, side-by-side, and thus offered an opportunity to disrupt common hierarchies and categories.

The corresponding recollection entries for each place were used as the sources of information for these seven datasets. While this was largely a process of copy-and-pasting information from the entries into the datasets, I had added a few presences to the recollected information when I felt that they ought to be present, as well as a few more indications of my own presence in space. The datasets were intended to feature three columns: “presence” (to name the co-inhabitant), “detail” (to provide more context, when applicable), and “category” (to organize by time/absence).

With the prepared datasets in hand, I attempted to build the sunburst chart code. This proved more difficult, yet also more simple, somehow, than I had anticipated. After unsuccessful attempts to refer to example code and to rely on Copilot assistance, I had realized that these data structures were simply incompatible with the construction of a sunburst chart. Thus, I had to rework the seven CSV files to match the chart’s logic of label-parent relationships. Once the datasets were structured in this way, I was able to refer to example chart code to quickly add functional code for the chart. Finally, as intended, the dashboard would construct, on-demand, a sunburst chart corresponding to a selection made in the map panel. At this point, I created a list of the remaining tasks, decided that visual adjustments and web page refinements could come after the initial draft was completed, and basked in the sense of accomplishment as that completion drew near.

Rather impulsively, I had taken some time to identify more suitable open-source basemaps for both this project and the second project. After unsuccessfully grappling with MapLibre’s Leaflet integration, I decided to look for new Leaflet map layers. I found this resource ([https://leaflet-extras.github.io/leaflet-providers/preview/](https://leaflet-extras.github.io/leaflet-providers/preview/)), offering a collection of various available basemap layers. I sought basemaps that would offer a simple and plain background, with less emphasis on roadways and (anthropocentric and colonial) place-names. Eventually, a few appropriate basemaps were identified, and among them, I settled upon the layers that are currently in use.

Next, it was time to write the text that would grace the dashboard. I began by crafting a statement about honoring relationality and my responsibility for (absent) absences. This statement included the responsibility to interrogate erasures. Next came the development of a project background draft, where I set up the intention, themes, and ethos of this project, hoping that, through doing so, I (and my audience) might be prompted to sit with thought-provoking, and at times challenging, questions. Suddenly, I found myself pondering the possible role of futurity in this project. How might I explore and understand the future of these places through past, present, and unnoticed presences?

Finally, the time had come to put the finishing touches on this project draft. I set the color scheme of the sunburst chart, opting for pastel yellow (to represent the present), blue (to represent the past), and purple (to represent the unnoticed, unknown, or even the future). The same hues of yellow (present) and blue (past) were used on the web map to set the base color of the polygons (blue) and the highlighted color (yellow). While I was not particularly keen on such a prominent use of yellow across the project design, the thematic rationale behind the color was more than enough to convince me. I then took some time to craft the finalized land acknowledgement for this project, drawing from what I had prepared for the second project and shaping it to the contours of this project. It was decided that the context panel could be a space where I might offer further information about each place, although I was uncertain as to what kind of information I might provide. To this day, placeholder text remains in this draft. Yet, when it comes time to prepare this work for a broader audience, it is quite likely that additional context will be necessary. Yet, would too much context be too me-centric, or too anthropocentric?

All that remained, now, was small tweaks. A note about the situated nature of this project and its absences was added. Ruminations upon the situated nature of this work could be a strong use of the space set aside for additional context, I reckoned. The seven CSV files were adjusted as I added text breaks to a few data values in an effort to improve the readability of the corresponding sunburst charts. I drafted a project title, as well as a dashboard title and map title, too. There are slight differences between the project title (represented in the webpage title) and the dashboard title, although I did wonder if the title is a bit too subtle regarding the key themes that underlie this project. Again, I had questioned whether or not place was truly explicit in this project; while place is referenced, it does not feel quite as prominent in the ontological underpinnings of this project as it does in those of the previous projects. While the dashboard may not be quite as unconventional as perhaps it could have been, given some of the more abstract considerations raised throughout this process, the initial draft of this third project was, at least, officially complete.
